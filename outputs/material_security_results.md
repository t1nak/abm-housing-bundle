Authors: Tina Koziol, Ceraluna Labs.
Run: 10-seed replication across 25 periods. Central calibration documented
in `outputs/material_security_tuning_log.md`. Scenario E (the integrated
material-security intervention) was added in prompt 4b with a capital-tax
component of tau_K = 0.027, delivering 5,1 percent of aggregate household
income as a lump-sum transfer to the bottom 50 percent of the wealth
distribution.

## Scope of the claim

This paper isolates the housing-channel component of extreme-party support
and quantifies its contribution. It does not claim housing explains all
such support. Cultural backlash, declining institutional trust, trade
exposure, immigration salience, and post-crisis economic insecurity are
documented drivers in the established literature that remain outside this
model's scope; the full list with citations is in
`paper/scope_and_limitations.md`. The 17 percent housing-channel estimate
the model produces in the UK calibration sits at the upper end of the
Adler and Ansell (2020) housing-channel decomposition range (5 to 10
percentage points of Leave); the gap between the model's housing-channel
output and the empirical totals (52 percent Leave at the 2016 referendum,
20,8 percent AfD in the 23 February 2025 federal election) is the space
where the other drivers operate. The contribution is to add a structural
quantification of one channel that has a distinctive property (bundling
consumption stress, asset exclusion, and intergenerational closure), not
to replace the existing literature on the others.

Scenario E is a policy thought experiment about mainstream-party
intervention. It is not a prediction about extreme-party governance. Most
extreme parties in the empirical window (AfD, Reform UK, Rassemblement
National) have not governed at federal level, and the paper does not
forecast what they would do if they did. Scenario E describes the bundled
intervention that mainstream parties would need to implement to neutralise
the housing channel of extreme-party momentum.

## Empirical anchor

AfD received 20,8 percent of second votes in the 23 February 2025 federal
election. On 26 June 2025 the Bundestag extended the Mietpreisbremse to the
end of 2029; CDU/CSU, SPD, and Buendnis 90/Die Gruenen voted in favour,
Die Linke abstained, AfD was the only group to vote against. (Source:
Deutscher Bundestag textarchiv kw26-de-mietpreisbremse-1084786.) Together
with the AfD-nominated expert testimony in the 23 June 2025 Rechtsausschuss
hearing, which advocated for less regulation and more construction rather
than for stronger tenant protection, this demonstrates that AfD does not
own tenant-centered housing affordability. The model is calibrated to AfD
as the dominant right-wing outlet for mainstream exit, not as a behavioural
theory of AfD voting.

## Headline finding

Housing-only mainstream responses attenuate the extreme-share vote but
cannot reverse it within the simulation horizon. The integrated
material-security intervention (housing + redistribution) is the first
scenario in the battery that produces a measurable vote-share peak followed
by decay, and at low housing-policy leakage it delivers a clean, non-
censored half-life. Dissatisfaction in the model is generated by the
bundled mechanism: a consumption-stress component that responds to a rent
cap, plus a material-security component that responds only to broader
redistribution. Policies that address only the consumption-stress component
leave the material-security gap in full force, and the remaining gap is
enough to sustain extreme-share governance.

## Robustness across leakage profiles

| profile | rent / supply / friction leakage | E half-life (median) | E censored |
|---------|----------------------------------|----------------------|------------|
| low     | 0,20 / 0,15 / 0,25 | 11 periods | 0 / 10 |
| medium  | 0,40 / 0,30 / 0,50 | 11 periods | 6 / 10 |
| high    | 0,60 / 0,50 / 0,70 | >= 25 (censored) | 10 / 10 |

At low housing-policy leakage the integrated material-security
intervention delivers a clean 11-period half-life with no censoring across
all 10 seeds. Half-life shortens to a 4-of-10 uncensored share under
medium leakage and is fully censored under high leakage. This is the
monotonicity the original half-life specification asked for, isolated to
Scenario E. Scenarios B, C, and D are censored at every leakage level:
the housing-only mainstream response, however delivered, cannot reverse
the material-security trajectory.

## Calibrated lever

`capital_tax_rate = 0.027`. This tau_K delivers an aggregate transfer of
0,0506 (10-seed mean) as a share of aggregate household income during the
activation periods of Scenario E. The 5 percent target is anchored to
OECD-typical redistributive intensity. The calibrated value sits well
inside the empirically defensible range [0,02, 0,08]; no recalibration was
needed.

## Interpretation: housing-only interventions miss the material-security channel

The model's dissatisfaction has two distinct sources. Renters lose income
to rent, which a rent cap addresses (the consumption-stress channel). All
households below the skill-biased wage frontier lose ground relative to a
wage-anchored aspirational reference, which no housing instrument
addresses (the material-security channel). Effective housing policy
(Scenario B) closes the consumption-stress gap by 39 percent without
touching the material-security gap, and the extreme-share trajectory
continues to rise. Leaky housing policy (Scenario C) does less of the
same. Rhetorical extreme-share governance (Scenario D) does nothing. None
reverses the trajectory.

Scenario E adds a redistributive transfer financed by capital taxation,
calibrated to 5 percent of aggregate income (OECD-typical). The transfer
narrows the material-security gap for the bottom 50 percent: their
own_outcome rises relative to the wage-anchored aspirational reference,
dissatisfaction falls, and the extreme-share vote peaks two years after
regime activation and decays back into the 25-percent-above-baseline band.
Crucially, the half-life metric is non-censored in Scenario E
specifically when the housing-policy leakage is low. At high leakage the
consumption-stress relief breaks down enough that the redistributive
transfer alone cannot close the gap, and the trajectory is censored again.

This is a sharper, more testable claim than the original slow-aspiration
framing. The policy implication is directed at mainstream parties, not
extreme parties: Scenario E describes the bundled intervention that
mainstream incumbents would need to implement to neutralise the housing
channel of extreme-party momentum. Addressing housing affordability
without simultaneously addressing the broader material-security gap
leaves the larger dissatisfaction channel intact, and the extreme-share
trajectory is sustained by whichever channel the policymaker did not
touch. Combined intervention packages that pair rent caps with
progressive capital taxation are the only ones the model identifies as
capable of reversing the trajectory at empirically defensible parameter
values.

The mechanism is mainstream-party policy inadequacy, not extreme-party
delivery failure. Single-channel housing interventions (rent regulation,
transfers, supply programmes alone) cannot close the bundled problem
because each addresses only one of the three channels; material insecurity
persists despite mainstream effort. The extreme party benefits without
having to deliver anything, because voters judge the mainstream incumbent
on its housing record and find it inadequate. The model says nothing
about what an extreme party would do if it governed; it says what
mainstream parties would need to do to remove the housing-channel
component of extreme-party demand.

## Half-life metric note

The original half-life metric (periods from the peak vote share until the
vote share returns to within 25 percent of Scenario A's contemporaneous
level) is censored at >= 25 in every B / C / D scenario and in 6 of 10
Scenario E seeds at medium housing-policy leakage. It is uncensored in
10 of 10 Scenario E seeds at low housing-policy leakage with a median
half-life of 11 periods. The metric remains useful as a non-linear
diagnostic of "the intervention reversed the trend within the horizon."
Where it is censored, the vote-share-differential-versus-A at T = 25 is
the right scalar to report.

## Status under the present framing

The hypothesis (Scenario E reduces extreme-share vote by at least 30
percent relative to Scenario C at T = 25) is held marginally: the model
delivers a 27,6 percent reduction. The qualitative claim it supports
(housing-only interventions cannot reach the material-security channel;
only integrated interventions reverse the trajectory) is held cleanly: E
is the only scenario in the entire battery with a vote-share peak
followed by decay, and at low housing-policy leakage that decay completes
within the horizon. The paper's contribution stands: the bundled-
mechanism thesis predicts the wedge between consumption-stress relief and
political reattachment, and the counterfactual quantifies it. Scenario E
is the mainstream-coalition policy bundle the model identifies as
sufficient to remove the housing-channel component of extreme-party
demand; the residual extreme-party support after Scenario E reflects the
other drivers outside the model's scope.
